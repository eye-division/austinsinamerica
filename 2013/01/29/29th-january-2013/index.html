Austins in Americas

Inspired by John Coleman's drive in 1959, five crew will drive three Austin 7's the 11,000 miles from Buenos Aires to New York City through twelve countries and two continents. Arrival in Times Square is planned for noon on May 31st 2013. Read the blogs below for progress and news.

29th January 2013

Posted on January 29, 2013 by jackp

The trip plaque

 

Veronica Garside, says goodbye to the chummy

Since the last posting much time and energy has been spent on getting the cars finished and rearranging the trip logistics. The cars were eventually accepted as Ro-Ro cargo, a welcome change from the containerisation that was the only option from September onwards. This has saved a lot of time, money and worry. However the shipping date was brought forward by the shipping line and so we elected to go on a later ship to avoid the cars sitting on a dockside in Argentina before we arrived. This resulted in River having to change his flight out to join us and the rest of the party will now enjoy the delights of Buenos Aires for a little longer than was originally planned. I for one will be welcoming some recovery time before we set off and Buenos Aires is a good place to enjoy the sunshine and warmth.

Diana’s mother Veronica, stores the chummy at the family home in Wiltshire, where I collected it for a few last minute jobs and to deliver to the ship. It started on the handle and drove beautifully back to Oxford, where it shared accommodation with our RP for a few days.

I had been getting around to those jobs which had seemed like an easy number when listed back in the spring, now they assumed gigantic proportions as the time dwindled to nothing and the deadline approached at a gallop. Unexpected catastrophes also appeared, specially made brake parts were delayed by sickness, Christmas and postal delays. The head gasket failed on New Years Eve, Chris Parkhurst bustled over from Aylesbury with a replacement head, gasket and compression tester and within a few hours the Box was back running. We made it to the Stoney Stratford New Years Day meeting without incident. Later the engine and gearbox had to be removed and repairs made in quick order with the assistance of David Williams. The opportunity was taken to adjust aspects of how the engine fitted in the chassis and the car is much better for the expert fettling it received in Bradford-on-Avon.

The RP sunshine roof being repaired

I managed another three or four hundred miles running on the rebuilt front axle and adapted full Girling front brake system and the car is now running better than before. The night before Tilbury however during an oil change the replacement filter cartridges were found to be the wrong type and the new 12 volt indicator unit failed after only a week.

The list of jobs gradually reduced as the time got shorter, finally time ran out and it was the day of delivery. Stan had finished his car at midnight the night before and we joined up on the M25 near the M11 junction and proceeded in convoy to Tilbury. Amanda towed the chummy on a trailer and the RP purred around the M25 at a steady 50, even overtaking one or two lorries on the hilly sections. It was Thursday 18th January and we left the cars, having booked them in, in the compound at Dock 4 Tilbury. Amanda and I returned on Monday 21st, after a snowy weekend and all the cars started with very little trouble. We followed the Port Captains car onto the loading ramp and then up the steep internal ramps to 7 decks above. Clean, warm, well lit and spotlessly maintained, the inside of the ship was like a new multi storey car park.

Austins await boarding at Tilbury in the snow. The Grande Costa D’Avoria, looking like a floating block of flats, in the background

A second journey with the third car saw them all safely strapped down and petrol and sparks turned off for the duration. I only hope the oil drips will not be too noticeable on the clean decks. We had been asked to go during the loaders morning break to avoid their high speed loading of new cars, ours were tucked in to small spaces between the brash new ones, but looked charming amongst the gleaming paintwork.

Packed to the roof, the cars carry spare tyres for friends in Argentina with Austins, spares for our journey, camping equipment and basic food rations in case of emergency. Stan’s saga of woes, had included a troublesome distributor set up, relining the brakes, a strong box for the back seat area and a replacement fuel tank. All of which involved suppliers and spares remote to Cumbria, where he lives, so many motorway miles and late nights.

Dispatch label

The American part of the group were meantime pursuing insurance in South and Central America, finally solved by a friendly German motorist who runs a nominal fleet of vehicles, owned by his International contacts, from his address in El Bolson in Argentina, a contact made on our last pass through Argentina in 2009. This will suffice to cover Argentina and Chile, we will probably be able to buy insurance of a sort at borders from there on. We hope to be able to benefit from Guy Butchers present travels in the opposite direction in an Austin 7.

So now we are dealing with the last paperwork items, paying the shipping and insurance bills and counting the days until we leave for Heathrow. Stan will stay with us in Oxford overnight before and we are to be met at the airport in Buenos Aires by Mac and Phyllis, who have been working on our behalf in Argentina. Our house sitters will arrive on the 1st February, by which time I hope to have finished an additional bedroom and bathroom for them, so no let-up in the pressure then!!!
